## Policy Brief: QSL Engagement with ProQure Vendors

### Framing Decision

- **Reject the binary**: DSIT's "full separation" position treats this as conflict-of-interest vs. clean separation — a false dichotomy; the real axis is *governed engagement* vs. *ungoverned exclusion*
- **Correct the procurement instinct**: UK procurement law (PCR 2015) requires *defensible process*, not institutional purity; independence is a procedural property, not an organisational one
- **Precedent architecture**: DARPA TAOs retain industry relationships; ESA's ESTEC evaluates vendors it co-develops standards with; CERN procurement routinely uses researchers embedded in vendor programmes — the model exists

---

### 1 — Full Separation Is Not Workable

**Key decision: Reject the workability of full separation**

- QATCH has 32 named partners, ≥6 of whom are probable ProQure Phase 1 applicants (IBM, Quantinuum, ORCA, Rigetti, Phasecraft, Algorithmiq)
- QATCH deliverables — Testbed Technical Evaluation toolkit, Resource Estimation tool, Verification-Inspired Benchmarking — are *co-produced* with these vendors; separation severs the deliverable chain mid-stream
- The TTE programme already produces cross-testbed benchmarking outputs that will inform ProQure evaluation whether QSL is formally involved or not; exclusion creates *informal* influence without *formal* accountability
- **Tradeoff**: Separation *appears* cleaner but actually produces a worse conflict-of-interest outcome — QSL's methodology permeates UK quantum evaluation regardless; better to surface it under governance than suppress it into grey zones

---

### 2 — Methodology Transfer: Credible Neutrality or Two-Tier System?

**Key decision: Methodology transfer is credible *if and only if* the methodology is published before procurement assessment begins**

- The model — develop benchmarking through deep collaboration with some vendors, apply it uniformly to all — is standard in standards bodies (IEEE, ETSI) and test agencies (NPL, PTB)
- The two-tier *perception* problem is real: IBM and Quantinuum helped design the benchmarks; a startup from outside QSL's orbit did not
- **Resolution pattern**: Pre-publish all methodology (Zero-Trust Verification framework, Entropy Benchmarking toolkit, Resource Estimation parameters) as open specifications *before* ProQure Phase 1 assessment begins; any vendor can review, comment, and validate against their own systems
- This converts the two-tier perception into a public consultation record — legally defensible and scientifically reproducible
- **Architectural tradeoff**: Publishing methodology early constrains QSL's research flexibility but is the price of procurement participation

---

### 3 — Governance Structure for DSIT Confidence

**Key decision: Three-layer governance with hard separation of roles**

**Layer 1 — Structural role split**
- QSL function in ProQure: *Technical Evidence Provider* only — no scoring, no ranking, no recommendation
- Scoring and vendor selection: Independent Assessment Panel (5 assessors, none from QSL/NQCC orbit)
- QSL delivers: raw benchmark data, verification reports, resource estimation outputs — all auditable artefacts
- Kashefi recuses from any ProQure governance role in her NQCC Chief Scientist capacity; NQCC appoints a separate ProQure Technical Lead

**Layer 2 — Chinese wall mechanisms**
- QSL's ProQure evaluation function sits in a dedicated *Evaluation Unit* staffed by researchers with no active QATCH vendor-facing roles
- Evaluation Unit personnel cannot hold co-authorship, co-PI positions, or consulting arrangements with any ProQure applicant during the evaluation window (Oct 2026 – Phase 2 award)

**Layer 3 — Independent oversight**
- Standing Technical Oversight Committee: DSIT nominee, Innovate UK nominee, one international academic (non-UK), one industry nominee from a *non-applicant* vendor
- TOC reviews QSL methodology before deployment and any QSL finding that materially differentiates vendors
- TOC has veto over QSL output inclusion in assessment pack if process violated

### 4 — ProQure Needs Its Own Evaluation Budget

**Key decision: This is the strongest structural fix and the hardest sell internally**

- QATCH's £20M is EPSRC research funding — it cannot legally be redirected to government procurement evaluation
- If ProQure deploys £140M in Phase 1 alone, and the evaluation infrastructure relies on unfunded goodwill from QSL, the programme is architecturally fragile
- **Proposed model**: ProQure allocates 1-2% of contract value (~£1.4-2.8M Phase 1, scaling to £15M over the programme) as a dedicated *Independent Technical Evaluation* budget line
- This funds: Evaluation Unit personnel, external peer review of QSL outputs, international benchmark calibration (with e.g. Sandia National Labs, PTB Berlin), and the TOC secretariat
- **Tradeoff**: Adds procurement overhead; Innovate UK will resist; but it transforms QSL from an *ad hoc dependency* into a *contracted evaluation function* — legally cleaner, institutionally sustainable
- Without this, QSL participation is a favour that can be withdrawn; with it, it's a service with defined scope and liability

**Key decision: Frame the ask as risk mitigation, not institutional advocacy**

**The legal risk argument**
- Exclusion of QSL does *not* reduce legal risk — it relocates it; if the evaluation methodology used by substitute assessors is demonstrably weaker, losing vendors challenge the *quality* of assessment, not just the process
- Weak evaluation of a £1B programme is a judicial review target; QSL's exclusion creates the very vulnerability DSIT is trying to avoid
- UK procurement law requires *proportionate and competent* evaluation; DSIT must demonstrate the alternative is at least as competent

**The counterfactual stress test** (this is the killer argument)

**The positive ask**
