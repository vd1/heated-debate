## Policy Brief: QSL Engagement with ProQure Vendors

### Framing Decision

- **Reject the binary**: DSIT's "full separation" position treats this as conflict-of-interest vs. clean separation — a false dichotomy; the real axis is *governed engagement* vs. *ungoverned exclusion*
- **Correct the procurement instinct**: UK procurement law (PCR 2015) requires *defensible process*, not institutional purity; independence is a procedural property, not an organisational one
- **Precedent architecture**: DARPA TAOs retain industry relationships; ESA's ESTEC evaluates vendors it co-develops standards with; CERN procurement routinely uses researchers embedded in vendor programmes — the model exists

---

### 1 — Full Separation Is Not Workable

**Key decision: Reject the workability of full separation**

- QATCH has 32 named partners, ≥6 of whom are probable ProQure Phase 1 applicants (IBM, Quantinuum, ORCA, Rigetti, Phasecraft, Algorithmiq)
- QATCH deliverables — Testbed Technical Evaluation toolkit, Resource Estimation tool, Verification-Inspired Benchmarking — are *co-produced* with these vendors; separation severs the deliverable chain mid-stream
- The TTE programme already produces cross-testbed benchmarking outputs that will inform ProQure evaluation whether QSL is formally involved or not; exclusion creates *informal* influence without *formal* accountability
- **Tradeoff**: Separation *appears* cleaner but actually produces a worse conflict-of-interest outcome — QSL's methodology permeates UK quantum evaluation regardless; better to surface it under governance than suppress it into grey zones

---

### 2 — Methodology Transfer: Credible Neutrality or Two-Tier System?

**Key decision: Methodology transfer is credible *if and only if* the methodology is published before procurement assessment begins**

- The model — develop benchmarking through deep collaboration with some vendors, apply it uniformly to all — is standard in standards bodies (IEEE, ETSI) and test agencies (NPL, PTB)
- The two-tier *perception* problem is real: IBM and Quantinuum helped design the benchmarks; a startup from outside QSL's orbit did not
- **Resolution pattern**: Pre-publish all methodology (Zero-Trust Verification framework, Entropy Benchmarking toolkit, Resource Estimation parameters) as open specifications *before* ProQure Phase 1 assessment begins; any vendor can review, comment, and validate against their own systems
- This converts the two-tier perception into a public consultation record — legally defensible and scientifically reproducible
- **Architectural tradeoff**: Publishing methodology early constrains QSL's research flexibility but is the price of procurement participation

---

### 3 — Governance Structure for DSIT Confidence

**Key decision: Three-layer governance with hard separation of roles**

**Layer 1 — Structural role split**
- QSL function in ProQure: *Technical Evidence Provider* only — no scoring, no ranking, no recommendation
- Scoring and vendor selection: Independent Assessment Panel (5 assessors, none from QSL/NQCC orbit)
- QSL delivers: raw benchmark data, verification reports, resource estimation outputs — all auditable artefacts
- Kashefi recuses from any ProQure governance role in her NQCC Chief Scientist capacity; NQCC appoints a separate ProQure Technical Lead

**Layer 2 — Chinese wall mechanisms**
- QSL's ProQure evaluation function sits in a dedicated *Evaluation Unit* staffed by researchers with no active QATCH vendor-facing roles
- Evaluation Unit personnel cannot hold co-authorship, co-PI positions, or consulting arrangements with any ProQure applicant during the evaluation window (Oct 2026 – Phase 2 award)
- Audit trail: all vendor interactions during evaluation period logged and disclosed to Innovate UK on request

**Layer 3 — Independent oversight**
- Standing Technical Oversight Committee: DSIT nominee, Innovate UK nominee, one international academic (non-UK), one industry nominee from a *non-applicant* vendor
- TOC reviews QSL methodology before deployment and any QSL finding that materially differentiates vendors
- TOC has veto over QSL output inclusion in assessment pack if process violated

---

### 4 — ProQure Needs Its Own Evaluation Budget

**Key decision: This is the strongest structural fix and the hardest sell internally**

- QATCH's £20M is EPSRC research funding — it cannot legally be redirected to government procurement evaluation
- If ProQure deploys £140M in Phase 1 alone, and the evaluation infrastructure relies on unfunded goodwill from QSL, the programme is architecturally fragile
- **Proposed model**: ProQure allocates 1-2% of contract value (~£1.4-2.8M Phase 1, scaling to £15M over the programme) as a dedicated *Independent Technical Evaluation* budget line
- This funds: Evaluation Unit personnel, external peer review of QSL outputs, international benchmark calibration (with e.g. Sandia National Labs, PTB Berlin), and the TOC secretariat
- **Tradeoff**: Adds procurement overhead; Innovate UK will resist; but it transforms QSL from an *ad hoc dependency* into a *contracted evaluation function* — legally cleaner, institutionally sustainable
- Without this, QSL participation is a favour that can be withdrawn; with it, it's a service with defined scope and liability

---

### 5 — Convincing DSIT: The Concrete Argument

**Key decision: Frame the ask as risk mitigation, not institutional advocacy**

DSIT's current position is driven by two fears: legal challenge from losing vendors, and reputational damage from perceived insider dealing. Address both directly:

**The legal risk argument**
- Exclusion of QSL does *not* reduce legal risk — it relocates it; if the evaluation methodology used by substitute assessors is demonstrably weaker, losing vendors challenge the *quality* of assessment, not just the process
- Weak evaluation of a £1B programme is a judicial review target; QSL's exclusion creates the very vulnerability DSIT is trying to avoid
- UK procurement law requires *proportionate and competent* evaluation; DSIT must demonstrate the alternative is at least as competent

**The counterfactual stress test** (this is the killer argument)
- Name the alternative: which institution has cross-platform benchmarking capability across superconducting, ion-trap, neutral-atom, photonic, and annealing systems?
- The honest answer is: none in the UK; international alternatives (Sandia, NIST, Fraunhofer) are not subject to UK procurement law and add jurisdictional complexity
- A credible substitute requires 18-24 months to build; ProQure Phase 1 starts October 2026; the counterfactual is not "use someone else" — it's "evaluate badly or delay the programme"

**The positive ask**
- DSIT approves a *governed engagement model* along the lines above
- In exchange: QSL accepts formal constraints (Evaluation Unit ring-fence, Kashefi recusal, public methodology, TOC oversight)
- The agreement is written as a Memorandum of Understanding between DSIT, Innovate UK, NQCC, and University of Edinburgh — making governance commitments legally binding, not advisory

---

### Key Unresolved Tensions

- **Kashefi dual role**: No governance architecture fully resolves the concentration of expertise and conflict in one person; the recusal mechanism reduces but doesn't eliminate the optics problem; the long-term fix is succession planning for the NQCC Chief Scientist role
- **Methodology publication timing**: Early publication is governance-positive but competitively costly — QSL's benchmarking IP is also its research differentiation; this tension needs explicit negotiation with EPSRC
- **Evaluation budget source**: Whether ProQure or NQCC carries this cost is a political question about ownership of the evaluation function that DSIT, Innovate UK, and UKRI have not yet resolved

---

**Bottom line:** The case for governed engagement is stronger than the case for exclusion on every dimension — legal defensibility, evaluation quality, programme timeline, and cost. DSIT's position is a risk-avoidance reflex, not a considered procurement design. The task is to give DSIT a governance structure it can defend, not to argue that conflict of interest doesn't exist.

**Critical Flaws**
- The legal frame is wrong. For a call opened on 27 March 2026 and closing on 29 May 2026, the live baseline is the UK [Procurement Act 2023](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/procurement-act-2023-guidance-documents-define-phase/guidance-conflicts-of-interest-html), not PCR 2015. That matters because the Act formalises a conflicts assessment and can require exclusion where an unfair advantage cannot be neutralised.
- The proposal collapses three different problems into one: bid-stage assessment in ProQure Phase 1, post-award technical verification during contract delivery, and any future procurement beyond 2030. DSIT may accept different rules at each stage. A single governance model for all three is too blunt.
- “QSL provides evidence but does not score” is not a clean separation. Under current guidance, external experts who can influence decisions are still part of the conflict problem. If QSL chooses workloads, thresholds, failure modes, or interpretation rules, it is already shaping the outcome.
- The methodology-transfer defense is too optimistic. Publishing methods helps, but it does not erase tacit advantage from co-development: tooling choices, calibration habits, workload selection, debug intuition, and informal knowledge of vendor architectures. The two-tier problem remains.
- The Chinese-wall proposal is likely performative. If the real expertise sits in the same lab, same leadership chain, and same vendor network, the wall will look porous. If the wall is genuinely strict, DSIT will ask whether the remaining ring-fenced unit is still the “only credible evaluator.”
- The oversight design is risky. A Technical Oversight Committee with a non-applicant industry nominee creates a fresh confidentiality and market-intelligence problem. Giving that committee veto power over evidence also creates a shadow decision-maker outside the formal procurement process.
- The DARPA precedent is misused. Official DARPA practice cuts the other way: support contractors assisting evaluation are separated from technical performers, and DARPA bars concurrent advisory-support and performer roles in key cases. That weakens the “active neutrality has precedent” argument.
- The MoU idea is too soft. DSIT will care less about a memorandum and more about what is frozen into the procurement documents, conflict assessments, notices, contracts, and audit trail.

**Stronger Architecture**
- Reframe the ask. Do not ask DSIT to bless “QSL engagement with vendors” in general. Ask DSIT to permit a tightly bounded QSL role by procurement phase.
- Pre-call phase: QSL can help build public benchmark methods, but only through declared preliminary market engagement, with published outputs and a frozen protocol before bid evaluation starts.
- Live competition phase: QSL should be excluded from bidder-specific advisory contact, scoring, ranking, and any mid-stream methodology changes. Full separation from vendors is not workable for QATCH; full separation from source selection is.
- Post-award verification phase: QSL can run fixed technical tests against awarded testbeds, but only with replication or sign-off by a second independent body. That is where QSL’s capability is most defensible.
- Future large-scale procurement: ProQure should fund a separate evaluation function, ideally competed or constituted as a consortium. If DSIT pays for evaluation, it should not default that budget straight into QSL.

**Radical Alternatives**
- Airlock model: second QSL staff into a legally separate evaluation vehicle with cooling-off restrictions before they can return to vendor-facing QATCH work. Expensive and politically awkward, but more credible than an internal wall.
- Dual-key model: QSL runs the benchmark harness; an international lab or independent commercial evaluator replicates a subset and signs the evidence pack. This buys defensibility at the cost of speed and money.
- Blind-evaluation model: vendors expose systems through a common interface and are anonymised during initial technical assessment. Imperfect, but it reduces prestige and relationship bias.
- Split the problem institutionally: QSL owns public methods and benchmark science; NQCC or another body owns contract verification; an external panel owns award decisions. No single actor should own all three.

**What Might Actually Move DSIT**
- Stop arguing “trust QSL.” Argue “minimise QSL discretion.”
- Concede the hard point: QSL cannot credibly be both catalyst and live assessor of the same vendors in the same phase.
- Offer DSIT a narrower permission: allow QSL in pre-competitive methodology and post-award verification, not in bid award.
- Make the counterfactual less self-defeating. “Only QSL can do this” sounds like dependence and capture. Better: “QSL is necessary for part of the pipeline, but not sufficient to govern it alone.”
