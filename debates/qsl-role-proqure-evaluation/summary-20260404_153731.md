### Best ideas

1. **"Minimise QSL discretion, don't argue trust"** — the rhetorical reframe that makes the whole brief DSIT-palatable. Concede the hard point first, then negotiate from a defensible position.

2. **Red-route model with automatic triggers** — vendor-specific conflict routing instead of all-or-nothing institutional exclusion. Three-condition test (active QATCH WP + same subsystem + personnel overlap) is precise enough to survive legal scrutiny.

3. **Capability escrow** — insurance policy that reduces DSIT's dependency anxiety. QSL deposits benchmark tools; others can run them if a conflict fires. Write-only deposit, immutable hashes, NQCC custody.

4. **s.81-83 counterfactual argument** — force DSIT to document in the conflicts assessment what alternative evaluation capability exists. If the answer is "none on the required timeline," blanket exclusion trades manageable conflict risk for unmanageable quality risk.

5. **Temporal split** — acknowledging that the 2026 call is already live (closes 29 May) and can only accommodate post-award QSL involvement. Full governance architecture applies to future rounds. This honesty is essential for credibility with DSIT.

### Weaknesses

- The brief is long and detailed — a 2-page executive summary for ministerial consumption is still needed
- No engagement with the political economy: who in DSIT made the separation decision, why, and what would change their mind beyond legal arguments
