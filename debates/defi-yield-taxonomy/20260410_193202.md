# The Case: Water is NOT Wet

**Core Argument:**
Wetness is a *relational property*—the state of having liquid adhered to a surface. Water cannot possess this property relative to itself. A substance cannot make itself wet.

## Arguments

- **Definitional**
  - Wetness: liquid clinging to a surface, creating adhesion/cohesion effects
  - Water has no "surface" to cling to itself—only internal cohesive forces
  - By definition, only *other materials* (skin, fabric, paper) become wet when exposed to water

- **Philosophical**
  - Self-reference problem: asking if water is wet is like asking "is a knife sharp *to itself*?" 
  - Sharpness exists as a relational property between blade and object
  - Similarly, wetness is a property *between* water and substrate

- **Physical**
  - Water molecules experience cohesive forces, but this isn't "wetness"—it's molecular polarity
  - Wetness requires: (1) liquid present, (2) something else for it to adhere to
  - Water alone satisfies only condition 1

## Tradeoffs

| Position | Strength | Weakness |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Water IS wet | Colloquially intuitive, matches casual usage | Philosophically incoherent (property of what, relative to what?) |
| Water is NOT wet | Logically defensible, clarifies semantic confusion | Sounds pedantic; violates vernacular |

## Concrete Proposal

Reframe the question: **"Does water make things wet?"** (YES) vs. **"Is water itself wet?"** (NO). The first is empirically true and useful; the second commits a category error.
