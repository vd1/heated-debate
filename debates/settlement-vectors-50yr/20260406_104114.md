## Critique of the 3-Vector Model as a Settlement Decision Framework

### What the 3 vectors get right
- They are **person-facing**, not system-facing — each asks "what happens to *me* here?"
- They capture the three ways a location can fail you: it becomes *dangerous*, *uninhabitable*, or *economically hostile*
- They are orthogonal enough that a place can score well on one and catastrophically on another (e.g., Singapore: low violence, excellent capital position, acute ecological fragility)

### What's missing — and it's critical

**1. Optionality / Exit capacity** — The 3 vectors assume a one-shot decision. Over 50 years, the ability to *leave* may matter more than any static score.
- Passport strength, dual citizenship availability, geographic proximity to multiple viable regions
- A country that scores 7/10 on all three vectors but from which you *cannot leave* is worse than a 5/10 country with easy exit to three 8/10 countries
- **This is the single largest omission.** Settlement planning without exit planning is a trap.

**2. Adaptive governance / institutional plasticity** — Not the same as "low violence" or "state capacity." The question: *can this place change course when conditions shift?*
- Small democracies with functional feedback loops (Nordic countries, Uruguay, New Zealand) vs. large states with ideological rigidity or captured institutions
- Federal structures with subnational variation (Canada, Switzerland, Germany) offer internal optionality
- This dimension is why "current safety" is a poor proxy — what matters is the **derivative**, not the level

**3. Social integration / belonging cost** — Ignored by all three vectors but decisive for whether you *stay*
- Language barriers, cultural openness to immigrants, path to citizenship, ability to own property/land, professional credential recognition
- A location that scores perfectly on safety, ecology, and economy but where you remain a permanent outsider is not a settlement — it's an extended hotel stay
- **Class-mediated:** wealthy immigrants face lower belonging costs almost everywhere, but even wealthy outsiders face social exclusion in Japan, South Korea, Gulf states

**4. Knowledge/network proximity** — Where the people you need are
- 50-year horizon means your children's opportunities matter as much as yours
- Access to education, research institutions, cultural production, professional networks
- Remote work partially decouples this, but only partially — and the trend may reverse if AI makes remote cognitive labor less valuable

**5. Temporal sequencing** — The framework treats settlement as a single decision when it's actually a *path*
- Where to be in 2026-2035 (accumulation phase) may differ from 2035-2050 (positioning phase) and 2050-2075 (endurance phase)
- Energy transition timelines create windows: fossil states are still wealthy now but declining; renewable-rich regions are building but not yet mature

## Radical Challenges to the Premise

**Challenge 1: The "choose a country" frame is already obsolete for the people who can act on this analysis.**
- High-agency individuals and families with capital are better modeled as **multi-nodal**, not single-location
- The real decision is: what *portfolio of residencies, citizenships, and property positions* gives maximum robustness?
- Implication: score locations not just absolutely but by their **complementarity** — two mediocre locations that are uncorrelated in failure modes beat one excellent location

**Challenge 2: Community > Country.**
- Historical evidence from civilizational collapses (Western Rome, Soviet Union, Yugoslavia) shows that *local community resilience* dominated national-level outcomes
- A strong local network in a struggling country may outperform atomized affluence in a thriving one
- The framework should have a "social capital" dimension that is *personal*, not just a property of the location
- **Controversial implication:** for most people, the best 50-year settlement strategy is to stay where their community is deep and invest in making it resilient, not to optimize location

**Challenge 3: The carrying-capacity thesis implies *no location is safe* — only *rate of decline* varies.**
- If the root cause is genuine overshoot, the framework becomes a triage tool, not an optimization tool
- The honest question shifts from "where is good?" to "where degrades last and most gracefully?"
- This reframes the decision as: proximity to primary productivity (food, water, energy *at the local level*), not proximity to GDP

## Proposed Framework: 7 Dimensions for Settlement Scoring

Replace the 3-vector model with a **7-dimension model** grouped into three tiers:

### Tier 1 — Survival floor (binary: if any of these fail, location is disqualified)

| Dimension | Core question | Key indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical safety** | Will the state or neighbors kill/imprison me? | Conflict risk, authoritarianism trend, ethnic/religious targeting risk for *your specific profile* |
| **Biophysical habitability** | Can humans live here in 2075? | Water availability trajectory, wet-bulb temperature projections, food production capacity, sea level exposure |

### Tier 2 — Structural resilience (scored 1-10, weighted)

| Dimension | Core question | Key indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Institutional adaptiveness** | Can this place *change course*? | Democratic feedback quality, judicial independence, fiscal headroom, subnational autonomy |
| **Economic positioning** | Am I on the right side of the coming power shift? | Energy mix trajectory, AI/compute infrastructure, inequality trend (Gini derivative, not level), property rights durability |
| **Local carrying capacity** | Can this region feed/power itself if global supply chains fracture? | Agricultural self-sufficiency, renewable energy potential, water independence, population-to-arable-land ratio |

### Tier 3 — Quality of life over decades (scored 1-10, but only matters after Tiers 1-2 pass)

| Dimension | Core question | Key indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Integration viability** | Can I *belong* here? | Language accessibility, immigration culture, path to citizenship, professional mobility, cultural proximity |
| **Optionality** | Can I *leave* if this bet fails? | Passport/visa regime, geographic position (proximity to multiple viable alternatives), financial system openness, dual citizenship tolerance |

### How to use it

1. **Eliminate** on Tier 1. If either dimension is red, stop evaluating.
2. **Score** Tier 2 dimensions 1-10 with explicit weights based on your household's situation (e.g., a family with agricultural skills weights local carrying capacity higher; a software engineer weights economic positioning higher).
3. **Score** Tier 3. These are tiebreakers between Tier 2 finalists.
4. **Evaluate complementarity.** If choosing a portfolio (primary + fallback locations), anti-correlate failure modes. Don't pick two places that both fail if AMOC collapses, or both fail if US-China conflict escalates.
5. **Sequence temporally.** Score separately for 2026-2040 and 2040-2075. Accept that the second-phase location may not be the first-phase one.

## Key tradeoffs in the framework design

- **Parsimony vs. completeness:** 7 dimensions is more complex than 3, but 3 had gaps that could produce catastrophic misses (especially optionality and institutional adaptiveness)
- **Universal vs. personal:** Physical safety risk is *identity-dependent* (ethnicity, religion, sexuality, nationality). The framework must be parameterized per household, not per country.
- **Static scoring vs. trajectory scoring:** Every dimension should be scored on its *derivative* (improving/declining) not just current level. A 6/10 trending to 8 beats a 9/10 trending to 4.
- **Single-location vs. portfolio thinking:** The framework supports both but is most powerful when used to compose a portfolio of 2-3 locations with uncorrelated risk profiles.

## What the original 3 vectors map to

| Original vector | Maps to | What was added |
|---|---|---|
| Man vs man | Physical safety + Institutional adaptiveness | Split static safety from dynamic governance quality |
| Man vs ecological services | Biophysical habitability + Local carrying capacity | Split global climate exposure from local self-sufficiency |
| Capital vs work | Economic positioning | Narrowed and sharpened; AI/compute replaces generic "capital" |
| *(missing)* | Integration viability | Entirely new |
| *(missing)* | Optionality | Entirely new — arguably the most important addition |

The 3-vector model was a valid first cut. The 7-dimension model preserves its core insight (settlement planning is about surviving *conflict between humans over diminishing resources*) while adding the dimensions that actually determine whether a 50-year bet pays off: can you adapt, can you leave, and can you belong.
