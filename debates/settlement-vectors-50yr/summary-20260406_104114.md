# Settlement Decision Framework — Summary of Conclusions

## Core Finding

The original 3-vector scoring model (man vs man, man vs ecological services, capital vs work) is a valid first cut but has critical gaps for a 50-year settlement decision. The debate converged on a **5-part decision stack** with strict evaluation discipline, replacing static country-level scoring with household-specific, stress-tested, locality-level assessment.

## What the 3 Vectors Miss

| Gap | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Optionality / exit capacity** | The single largest omission. A place you cannot leave is worse than a mediocre place with easy exit to better alternatives. Over 50 years, the ability to leave may matter more than any static score. |
| **Household fit** | Settlement is a compatibility problem, not just a location problem. Skills, dependencies, identity risks, capital, and network determine everything downstream. |
| **Institutional adaptiveness** | Current safety is a poor proxy — what matters is whether the place can change course when conditions shift. The derivative, not the level. |
| **Local carrying capacity** | Can the region feed/power itself if global supply chains fracture? Distinct from global ecological trajectory. |
| **Integration / belonging** | Can you belong, or are you a permanent outsider? Under stress, the question is: do locals treat you as kin, client, or scapegoat? |
| **Extractability** | A place can be habitable and cohesive yet dangerous if stressed institutions, elites, or criminal actors can seize your assets, labor, or family members. |
| **Intergenerational continuity** | Can children enter the local status hierarchy? Will credentials transfer? Will property stay in-family? |
| **Dependency profile** | A family needing insulin, autism support, or stable digital banking is in a different decision universe than a healthy low-dependency household. |

## The Settlement Decision Stack (v3 Final)

### Evaluation order (fixed, sequential, gated)

**Part 0: Non-negotiables + agency budget** — Sovereign and eliminatory. Medical absolutes, legal absolutes, identity-risk absolutes, household cohesion, and the honest agency budget (money, time, admin complexity tolerance, disruption tolerance). Any failure kills the candidate immediately.

**Settlement unit lock** — Before full evaluation, specify the candidate to town/neighbourhood level minimum. Country-level is screening, not decision.

**Part 1: Who are we?** — Household inventory. Hard assets (capital, citizenships, property), capabilities (what's locally valuable under stress), dependencies (medical, educational, digital — ranked by supply chain link count), network depth, identity-specific risks.

**Branch into tracks:**
- **Track A (Portfolio):** Capital + multiple citizenships + portable income. Primary base + fallback + mobility rights, optimised for uncorrelated failure modes.
- **Track B (Rooting):** Anchored by network/resources. Deepen resilience where you are, become locally indispensable before stress arrives.
- **Track C (Staged Migration):** Currently B, building toward A over years.

Most frameworks assume Track A. Most people are Track B.

**Part 2: What role can we hold there?** — Dual posture model:
- *Public posture:* boring, legible, not worth extracting from.
- *Private posture:* useful and trusted within a small network.

Trap to avoid: defaulting to "mobile outsider" (easiest in peacetime, worst under stress — visible wealth + no local loyalty + exit routes closing).

**Part 3: How can this place fail us?** — Five failure modes:
- 3A: Biophysical failure (climate, water, food, habitability)
- 3B: Regime failure (authoritarianism, conscription, capital controls, targeting)
- 3C: Social failure (scapegoating, community fracture, ethnic/religious persecution)
- 3D: Infrastructure failure (grid, water systems, healthcare, transport, digital)
- 3E: Extraction failure (state, elite, criminal, kin/patron, gendered extraction)

**Historical stress readout** required: how did this locality behave during its last fiscal crisis, migration influx, blackout, political upheaval?

**Stress test** — Run each candidate through 7 personalised, adversarial scenarios:
1. Fiscal crisis
2. Migration surge (in which your household is reclassified as outsider/privileged)
3. Grid instability (+ your dependency profile)
4. Food shock (+ local network failure)
5. Authoritarian turn (+ capital controls, conscription)
6. Regional conflict
7. Biophysical threshold crossing

Verdicts per scenario: Survives / Stressed but viable / Must exit / Fatal. Any "Fatal" = reject. "Must exit" acceptable only if exit is viable in that scenario's conditions.

**Part 4: Can we leave?** — Legal/physical/financial exit capacity. Fallback node identified, shock correlation mapped between primary and fallback. Graceful degradation options: downgrade in place, split household temporarily, shift social posture. Family exit dimension: can the whole household leave, or do different passports/dependents create partial-exit dilemmas?

**Part 5: 5-year action plan** — Only for surviving candidates. Separates rehearsals from irreversible moves.

Rehearsals (pass/fail, not experiential):
- Healthcare use: filled prescription, saw specialist, used emergency services
- Bureaucracy completion: bank account, residency registration, tax filing
- Difficult-season tolerance: experienced worst month, household consensus
- Network formation: at least one person who takes your call in a crisis
- Dependency substitution: each top-3 dependency sourced locally
- Exit rehearsal: money moves out, fallback residency actionable, full-household logistics confirmed

Irreversible moves only after rehearsals pass.

## Discipline Constraints

**Evidence tags** on every major claim:
- `[observed]` — directly witnessed or primary source
- `[strong inference]` — multiple credible secondary sources
- `[unknown]` — not yet verified

Rule: >3 unknowns across Parts 2-4 freezes irreversible moves. Any unknown on a non-negotiable dependency, exit path, or extractor mechanism freezes immediately regardless of count.

**Trigger format** (five fields, no partial entries):
```
Indicator → Threshold → Action → Who decides → Time available
```

**Verdict language** (standardised):
- `reject` — fatal failure
- `viable only with fallback` — must-exit scenarios exist, fallback identified
- `viable with rehearsal` — passes on paper, unknowns need resolution
- `staging base` — intergenerationally non-continuous
- `home` — intergenerationally viable, full investment justified

**Intergenerational classification** (separate from verdict):
- `home` — children can inherit position and thrive
- `staging base` — invest in portability, not depth
- `harvest-and-exit` — extract value, plan next move

## Core Tradeoffs

- **Optionality vs. belonging:** The more mobile you remain, the longer you delay becoming hard to dislodge locally.
- **Visibility vs. value:** The more locally valuable you are, the easier you are to tax, extort, or conscript.
- **Dependency support vs. autonomy:** Places good for complex dependencies are often expensive, centralised, and easier to control.
- **Fertility of place vs. plunder risk:** A productive region under predatory governance is often worse than a less fertile region inside a dull, competent state.

## Red Team Question (required for every candidate)

> "In a bad decade, what makes us visible, valuable, and vulnerable here at the same time?"

## Where the Original 3 Vectors Ended Up

| Original vector | Maps to | What was added |
|---|---|---|
| Man vs man | Part 0 identity risk + Part 3B/3C regime & social failure | Split static safety from dynamic governance; added extraction and scapegoating |
| Man vs ecological services | Part 3A biophysical + Part 3D infrastructure | Split global climate from local self-sufficiency and infrastructure persistence |
| Capital vs work | Part 2 role strategy (household-specific) | Replaced macro inequality with capability fit and extractability |
| *(missing)* | Part 1 household inventory + track branching | The framework now starts with who you are, not where you're going |
| *(missing)* | Part 4 exit capacity | Elevated from afterthought to core question |
| *(missing)* | Part 5 rehearsals + intergenerational classification | Forced operational testing and generational honesty |
