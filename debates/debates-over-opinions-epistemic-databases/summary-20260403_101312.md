# Summary — Debates Over Opinions: Epistemic Databases

## Quality Score: 8/10

Strong debate with genuine intellectual progression. The original metaphor ("database of debates") was productively dismantled and rebuilt into a concrete specification. Both agents pushed each other — Agent B consistently identified under-specification, and Agent A responded with real structural upgrades rather than handwaving. Minor deduction: Agent A struggled with tooling permissions throughout, which occasionally broke the flow, and the final rounds became slightly repetitive as the spec converged.

## Key Conclusions

- **Individuals should not carry internal debate archives.** The cognitive cost is too high, and it privileges high-verbal System 2 thinkers over everyone else.
- **Opinions should gain one mandatory field: "what would change my mind?"** This is the minimum viable epistemic upgrade — opinions-plus-revision-triggers, not a new cognitive architecture.
- **Institutions, not individuals, should carry the debates.** Courts, peer review, prediction markets, red teams, and adversarial journalism are the natural homes for structured disagreement.
- **A three-layer architecture emerged:** individuals hold commitment records, trust networks enforce accountability, institutions preserve live adversarial debate.
- **Domain matters:** empirical, policy, and moral claims need different revision protocols, different confidence measures, and different enforcement mechanisms.

## Key Disagreements

- Whether the 9-field schema is lightweight enough for real adoption vs. already too heavy for anyone outside expert communities.
- Whether coalition-signaling opinions can ever be addressed by epistemic architecture (both agreed probably not, but didn't fully close the question).
- The degree to which institutional debate-carrying actually works in practice vs. in principle (courts suppress, journals distort, markets manipulate).

## Best Ideas

1. **Cache invalidation model for beliefs** — beliefs as L1 cache with revision triggers that invalidate and force re-evaluation. A genuinely novel reframe.
2. **"Who should carry the debates, and on whose behalf?"** — shifting from individual cognition to institutional design as the core question.
3. **Wager storage** as a complement to debate storage — commitments with skin in the game and feedback loops, at least for empirical claims.
4. **Domain-specific confidence types** — probability for empirical, robustness for policy, reflective stability for moral. Stops fake precision.
5. **Embarrassment infrastructure** — systems that make it mildly costly to ignore your own stated revision triggers. The missing middle between reputation and financial wagers.

## Weaknesses

- Agent A was repeatedly blocked by tooling permissions, leading to meta-commentary about the engine that diluted the intellectual content.
- The final spec was never actually written to file during the debate, so it exists only in scattered form across rounds 3-5.
- The institutional layer remained aspirational — five evaluation criteria were named but not operationalized with real-world scoring.
- The "opinions as social weapons" problem was acknowledged repeatedly but never seriously engaged. Both agents agreed it's outside scope, which may be too convenient.
