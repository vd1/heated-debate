## Epistemic Shift: Debates-as-Data vs. Opinions-as-Data

### Core Thesis Reframed
- Replace the internal data structure `Map<topic, opinion>` with `Map<topic, Debate[]>` — each person carries a *set of tensions*, not resolved positions
- Conversation becomes a **diff/merge of debate graphs**, not a clash of scalar beliefs
- This is not relativism ("all views equal") — it's **epistemic version control**: you track the *history of arguments*, not just the HEAD commit

### Arguments FOR (the Architect's position)

- **Complexity-adequate representation**: Modern policy problems (climate, AI safety, monetary policy) are genuinely multi-equilibrium — a single opinion *lossy-compresses* reality in ways that a debate faithfully preserves
- **Reduced identity fusion**: When "I believe X" becomes "I carry a debate about X," you decouple ego from position — reducing tribalism, motivated reasoning, and the sunk-cost of being publicly committed
- **Better Bayesian updating**: A debate-holder can absorb new evidence by *strengthening one side of an internal debate* rather than performing the psychologically expensive operation of *switching beliefs*
- **Richer social epistemics**: Two debate-holders meeting can do **argument grafting** — "your side B has a sub-argument I hadn't considered, let me import it" — rather than the binary agree/disagree
- **Decision-time flexibility**: When action is required, you *resolve* the debate contextually (this decision, this stakeholder set, this risk tolerance) rather than applying a cached opinion

### Arguments AGAINST (the Reviewer's position)

- **Action paralysis**: Beliefs are *compressed for a reason* — they're action-ready. A "database of debates" without a resolution protocol is just sophisticated indecision wearing a lab coat
- **Cognitive load is real**: Carrying N debates × M sides scales terribly. Opinions are a necessary lossy cache for bounded rationality. You're proposing everyone become a Supreme Court that never issues rulings
- **Social coordination degrades**: Coalitions, institutions, and movements require *shared commitments*, not shared ambivalences. "I carry a debate about whether democracy is good" is not a foundation for democratic participation
- **Infinite regress**: Meta-debates emerge immediately — "I carry a debate about whether I should carry debates" — and the framework offers no grounding axiom
- **Power asymmetry exploitation**: Sophisticated debate-carriers get out-maneuvered by people with clear, simple, wrong convictions. The debate-holder is still deliberating while the opinion-holder has already acted

### Radical Alternatives Neither Side Considers

- **Hybrid architecture: Debate + Decision Cache**: Like a CPU with L1/L2 cache — you carry the full debate (L2) but also maintain a *current best resolution* (L1) that gets invalidated when new arguments arrive. This is how good decision-makers already work, but unnamed
- **Debate provenance as social signal**: Instead of "I'm a liberal" or "I'm a libertarian," your identity becomes *which debates you've engaged with and how they're weighted*. This is richer than opinion-labels but compressible enough for coordination
- **Forking not merging**: When two debate-holders meet, they don't need to *merge* — they can **fork**. "I'm taking your version of the climate debate and running it alongside mine." This is closer to how intellectual influence actually works
- **Time-indexed debates**: Debates aren't static — they have vintages. "My 2024 debate on AI risk is different from my 2026 debate" acknowledges epistemic evolution without requiring belief-switching
- **The real unit isn't the debate — it's the *tension***: A debate is too structured. What people actually carry is *unresolved tensions* — felt contradictions that haven't been formalized. The database should be of *tensions*, with debates as one possible resolution format

### Philosophical Implications

- **Challenges correspondence theory of truth**: If the unit of knowledge is a debate rather than a proposition, truth becomes *the quality of the tension* rather than the correctness of the resolution
- **Rehabilitates dialectics**: Hegel's thesis-antithesis-synthesis, but without the teleological demand for synthesis. You can carry the thesis-antithesis indefinitely
- **Maps onto ensemble methods in ML**: Just as random forests outperform single decision trees, a "forest of debates" might outperform a "tree of opinions" for prediction and decision-making
- **Undermines the survey/poll epistemic model**: "What do Americans think about X?" becomes unanswerable if Americans carry debates, not opinions. This breaks a massive amount of political and social infrastructure

### Key Unresolved Tension

The deepest problem: **this framework may be self-defeating for collective action**. Individual decision-making might improve (more nuanced, less brittle, better calibrated), but collective decision-making requires *convergence* — and a society of debate-carriers may be a society that can never converge enough to act on anything at scale. The counter-argument is that our current opinion-based convergence is *fake convergence* (people agreeing on labels while meaning different things) and that explicit debate-carrying would produce *less but more real* consensus.

## The Revisable Commitment Schema

### Individual Layer: The Commitment Record

Four fields, as you suggested, plus two that earn their place:

| Field | Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **commitment** | Action-readiness statement | "I would vote for density-based zoning reform with sunset clauses" |
| **confidence** | Calibrated probability, 0–1 | 0.72 |
| **disconfirming conditions** | Specific, observable triggers for revision | "If 3+ natural experiments show no median rent decrease within 5yr of upzoning, I'd drop to 0.3" |
| **delegation source** | Who/what you're trusting, and why | "Deferring partly to Schleicher's land-use work; partly to Tokyo/Auckland natural experiments" |
| **domain tag** | {empirical, policy, moral} | policy |
| **last revised** | Date + trigger | "2026-02, after reading Duranton replication failure" |

What this gets you over opinions: the `disconfirming conditions` field is the only real innovation. Everything else is bookkeeping. But that one field does enormous work — it converts a static belief into a falsifiable contract with your future self. It's the `WHERE` clause on a database trigger: *when this condition fires, re-evaluate*.

What this gets you over debates: compression. You don't carry the full argument graph. You carry the *decision surface* — the point where arguments already collapsed into a position — plus the thin edge cases where new evidence could reopen it.

**The hard problem your critique identified — people naming conditions cheaply and ignoring them — requires three mechanisms:**

1. **Memory**: The commitment record must be persistent and retrievable. This is trivially solved by technology (an app, a journal, a prediction market profile) but culturally hard. People resist epistemic bookkeeping.

2. **Enforcement**: Not coercive, but *social*. If you publicly stated "I'd change my mind if X" and X happens, your community should be able to point at the record. This is what prediction markets partially achieve — your calibration history is public.

3. **Cost for non-updating**: The gentlest version is reputation. The strongest version is financial (wagers). The middle ground — and probably the most realistic — is *embarrassment infrastructure*: systems that make it mildly costly to ignore your own stated revision triggers. Peer review does this for scientists. Nothing does it for citizens.

### The Domain Problem

Your point about distinguishing domains is critical. The schema behaves differently depending on the tag:

**Empirical claims** (e.g., "mRNA vaccines reduce hospitalization by >60%"):
- Confidence is calibratable against base rates
- Disconfirming conditions can reference specific studies, data thresholds
- Wagers are the natural enforcement mechanism
- Delegation goes to scientific consensus, specific researchers, systematic reviews

**Policy claims** (e.g., "We should implement carbon pricing"):
- Confidence is partly empirical (will it reduce emissions?) and partly value-laden (is the distributional cost acceptable?)
- Disconfirming conditions split: the empirical part can have triggers; the value part cannot
- Decision rules under uncertainty matter more than precision — you need something like "given these plausible ranges, which policy has the best worst case?"
- Delegation goes to policy experts *for the empirical component* and to democratic process *for the value component*

**Moral claims** (e.g., "Factory farming is wrong"):
- Confidence maps to something more like "reflective stability" — how robust is this under perspective-taking?
- Disconfirming conditions are not empirical but dialogical: "If someone showed me a coherent moral framework where animal suffering doesn't count and I couldn't refute it, I'd lower confidence"
- Wagers don't apply. What applies is *value conflict procedures* — structured ways of exposing yourself to the strongest opposing moral intuitions
- Delegation is the most fraught: deferring to moral authorities has a terrible track record, but so does naive moral individualism

**This three-way split is the model's load-bearing wall.** Treating all commitments the same is the single biggest failure mode of both the original "database of debates" idea and of crude rationalist frameworks that try to put probabilities on everything. Some things are wagerable. Some things need decision theory. Some things need moral imagination. The schema field `domain_tag` routes you to the right protocol.

### Institutional Layer: Who Carries the Debates

Here's the core claim: **individuals should not maintain internal debate archives. Institutions should.**

| Level | Carries | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Individual** | Commitment records (the 6-field schema above) | Your personal position on AI regulation |
| **Trust network** | Uncertainty exchange — the audit trails behind commitments, shared among peers | A research group's internal disagreements about alignment approaches |
| **Adversarial institution** | Structured, persistent, live contestation | Courts, peer review, prediction markets, red teams, adversarial journalism |

The division of labor:

- **You** maintain commitments with revision triggers. This is cognitively cheap — it's opinions-plus-one-field.
- **Your peers** hold you accountable to your revision triggers and offer counter-evidence. This is what good intellectual communities already do, badly and informally.
- **Institutions** maintain the full adversarial structure: preserve minority positions, enforce argument quality, prevent premature closure, and keep the unresolved debates alive even when the public has moved on.

This maps to your interface design point: strangers see your commitments (the compressed layer), collaborators see your uncertainties (the audit trail), institutions preserve the full debate graph (the adversarial layer).

### What This Model Actually Changes

If adopted, the practical differences from status quo are small but pointed:

1. **Opinions gain one mandatory field**: "What would change my mind?" Every public position-statement, op-ed, policy recommendation, or social media take would be expected to include its revision conditions. This is a cultural norm change, not a technology change.

2. **Institutions get evaluated on debate preservation, not consensus production**. A court that produces a clear ruling *and* preserves a sharp dissent is doing its job. A scientific field that reaches consensus *and* maintains active replication efforts is doing its job. The metric shifts from "did you resolve the debate?" to "did you resolve the decision while keeping the debate alive?"

3. **The delegation chain becomes explicit.** Instead of "I believe in climate action," you say "I defer to IPCC AR6 on the physical science, to Nordhaus vs. Stern on the discount rate (unresolved, I lean Stern), and to democratic process on distributional tradeoffs." This is more honest, more revisable, and more useful to anyone trying to understand or challenge your position.

4. **"I don't know" gets structure.** Right now, uncertainty reads as weakness. Under this model, a well-specified uncertainty — "I'm at 0.55 on this with the following three considerations pulling each direction" — reads as rigor. The cultural norm shifts from penalizing uncertainty to penalizing *unstructured* certainty.

### Where This Model Is Still Weak

**The darker possibility you flagged — opinions as social weapons and loyalty badges — is the real limit.** The schema assumes people *want* to track truth. But many opinions exist to signal tribal membership, and their holders would actively resist attaching revision conditions because *the point is to be unconditional*. "I support X no matter what" is not a failed epistemology — it's a successful coalition signal. This model doesn't fix that. Nothing epistemic can. It's a political problem dressed in epistemic clothing.

**The bootstrapping problem**: The model works best when everyone uses it, but each early adopter pays a cost (more cognitive overhead, more vulnerability to "you said you'd update and you didn't") without proportional benefit. It's a coordination game with a bad equilibrium. Institutions could seed it — journals requiring "conditions for retraction" on papers, policy memos requiring "decision review triggers" — but organic adoption is unlikely.

**Memory inflation, again**: Not every commitment deserves the full schema. "I prefer chocolate over vanilla" doesn't need disconfirming conditions. The model needs a severity threshold — maybe only commitments that affect others, involve resource allocation, or enter public discourse earn the full record.
