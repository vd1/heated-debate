# RFC v0.1 Review — Seven-Dimension Assessment

## 1. "Built vs Designed" Gap: Restructuring Assessment

**Verdict: Major improvement, but the seam is still visible.**

- **What works:** The claim register (E1) is honest and well-structured. The DIG/YTD are credibly demonstrated with live outputs. The milestone table draws a clean line between "operational" and "grant deliverable."
- **What still leaks:**
  - The Abstract opens with "What We Have Built" but immediately pivots to "These tools already surface the structural signals that preceded the three most damaging Morpho incidents." This is a *retroactive claim about unbuilt scoring*, not a claim about the built tools. The DIG/YTD surface *structure*; they don't surface *signals* in any quantified sense until Z₁–Z₆ exist.
  - The retroactive case study (E3) says "The DIG **would have** flagged..." — this hedging is correct but sits in tension with the Abstract's more assertive framing. A reader scanning the Abstract gets a stronger impression than the evidence warrants.
  - The wsrUSD decomposition is genuinely impressive proof-of-work. But the risk *scorecard* at the bottom (smart contract risk: "4–5 layers", concentration: "85.8% BTC") is qualitative judgment, not model output. It reads like a model produced it. Needs a label: "Analyst assessment based on DIG/YTD output — not yet scored by the quantitative model."
- **Radical suggestion:** Open with the wsrUSD tree, not the incident narratives. Lead with the artifact, not the argument. Let the reader see the dependency graph first and *feel* the complexity before you tell them it's dangerous. The current structure argues → then proves. Invert it: prove → then argue.

## 2. Narrative & Public-Good Argument Strength

**Verdict: Strong but under-leveraging the best available ammunition.**

- **The Chaos Labs departure is a nuclear-grade rhetorical weapon and the draft doesn't use it.** The RFC was written before the April 6 announcement. It now needs to reference it. Specifically:
  - Chaos Labs ran at *negative margins for 3 years* at $5M+/year → $250K for a transparency layer is not ambitious spending, it's table stakes
  - "Risk is downstream of architecture" (Goldberg) → directly validates Morpho-native rather than generic
  - Aave's contributor exodus → Morpho should be *building* risk infrastructure now, not waiting for a crisis
- **Prosperi's article is cited but underused.** The 20–100x mispricing finding is mentioned once in the Abstract. It should anchor the Motivation section: "Independent research confirms the gap is not just qualitative but *quantitative* — depositors are receiving 5–10 basis points for exposures that rational pricing puts at 250–400 bps."
- **The public-good framing is correct but passive.** "Open-source upon delivery" is table stakes. The stronger argument: *no single curator is incentivized to build this because the value accrues to the ecosystem, not the builder.* This is a textbook public goods problem. Name it explicitly. The grant *is* the mechanism for funding non-excludable infrastructure.
- **LucaB's thread validates the cold-start problem** but the RFC doesn't reference curation economics at all. It should: "Bootstrapping a curation business requires a quant ($200–250K/yr), a dev, and biz dev — minimum three people. A $20K/yr risk feed is an order of magnitude cheaper than hiring in-house."
- **Missing:** The LlamaRisk proposal explicitly advocates "protocol-owned > delegated" risk infrastructure. Sigma Labs should frame itself as aligned with this emerging consensus, not just on Morpho but across DeFi.

## 3. Remaining Overclaims

**Verdict: Two material overclaims survive. Several soft ones.**

### Material overclaims:

1. **"These tools already surface the structural signals that preceded the three most damaging Morpho incidents."** — No. The DIG/YTD surface *structure*. The claim that this structure constitutes a "signal" requires the scoring layer (Z₁–Z₆) which doesn't exist yet. Correct framing: "These tools surface the structural dependencies from which risk signals can be derived once the scoring layer is operational."

2. **The retroactive case study implies detection capability that hasn't been demonstrated.** Saying "Z₅ would have flagged the hardcoded oracle" is a claim about a system that doesn't exist. The honest version: "The hardcoded oracle was readable on-chain. Our Z₅ factor is *designed* to penalise this configuration. We will validate this claim via incident replay backtesting in Month 4."

### Soft overclaims:

- "Live outputs covering hundreds of nodes per asset" — true for the specific examples shown (reUSD, wsrUSD). Unclear if this generalises to all 500+ Morpho collateral assets without manual intervention.
- "No manual categorisation, no hardcoded parameters" — the Z₃ smart contract similarity metric requires a reference database of ~3,050 exploited contracts. Who curates this database? How is it updated? This *is* a form of manual categorisation.
- "Cross-asset comparable by construction" — this is a design goal, not a demonstrated property. Φ-normalisation makes scores *formally* comparable but whether a Z₅ = 0.9 on a stablecoin and a Z₅ = 0.9 on an LST are *meaningfully* comparable requires calibration evidence that doesn't exist yet.

## 4. Budget & Milestone Credibility

**Verdict: Budget is credible for scope. Milestones are aggressive. One line item is a red flag.**

### Budget:
- $250K for 12 months / 2.5 FTE = ~$100K/FTE/year. For a French team with academic backgrounds, this is *below market* but feasible for a grant-funded project (not venture-backed salaries).
- **Red flag: "Team / Contributors: $25,000."** Research & Model Development is $95K and Smart Contract Infrastructure is $55K — *who does this work?* If the team is in "Team / Contributors" at $25K, that's $10K/person/year. If they're embedded in the functional categories, then "Team" is paying for what? This budget structure confuses cost categories (what you're building) with compensation (who's building it). Reviewers will notice. **Restructure as: personnel costs ($150K) + non-personnel ($100K) and show the mapping.**
- $35K for audits is thin but defensible if scoped to model methodology review rather than full smart contract audit.
- $10K for infrastructure/legal is realistic for a 12-month project using shared RPC providers and archival tools.

### Milestones:
- **Month 2: Z₁–Z₃ live on testnet** — aggressive. Z₃ (smart contract similarity scoring against 3,050-contract database) alone could take months of data engineering. Where does the exploit database come from?
- **Month 4: Full 6-factor + GARCH calibration** — requires enough historical data for MLE estimation. For newly listed tokens, the MAP estimator with category priors is specified — but the priors don't exist yet either.
- **Month 6: σe and intrinsic rating live** — requires cross-sectional distribution of σe across enough assets to set quantile boundaries. Feasible only if calibration in Month 4 covers 30+ assets.
- **Month 8: Merton PD + dashboard** — ambitious to ship both the hardest quantitative layer and a frontend in the same milestone.
- **Month 10: External audit** — good that it's gated. But who audits a GARCH/HMM risk model? The pool of qualified DeFi risk model auditors is ~3 firms globally.
- **Month 12: Mainnet + open-source** — contingent on everything above hitting timeline.

**Radical suggestion:** Add an explicit "what happens if we're 3 months late" clause. Every DAO grant in history has slipped. Addressing this proactively builds credibility. Propose a mechanism: if Tranche 2 milestones aren't met by January 2027, the team publishes a progress report and the community votes whether to release or redirect the remaining funds.

## 6. Credora Positioning

**Verdict: The current framing is diplomatic but strategically suboptimal.**

- "Complementary rather than competitive" is the safe play. But it creates a problem: **if Credora already provides risk ratings on Morpho, why fund a second system?**
- The RFC needs to answer this directly. The answer is in the draft but buried: Credora provides *point-in-time ratings* via expert consensus; Sigma Labs provides *continuous, on-chain-derivable scores* with full methodology transparency. These are architecturally different products.
- **The wstETH CAPO incident from LlamaRisk's post is the kill shot.** A closed-source risk oracle caused $1M in damages on Aave because nobody could inspect or override it. Credora's consensus methodology is not fully transparent either. The RFC should say: "We respect Credora's foundational work. Our contribution is methodological transparency — every score traceable to an on-chain read — which we believe is a necessary complement to consensus-based approaches."
- **What the RFC shouldn't do:** Attack Credora directly. RedStone acquired them; RedStone provides oracles to Morpho. Antagonising the oracle provider is poor politics.
- **Radical alternative:** Don't position against Credora at all. Position against *the absence of continuous risk monitoring*. The real competitor isn't Credora — it's "curators doing their own ad hoc analysis with no shared methodology." Frame the RFC as building the shared methodology layer, with Credora as one possible consumer of the framework's outputs.

**Verdict: The dual-tier model is the best idea in the entire RFC. But it needs stress-testing.**

### Strengths:
- Public/professional tier split is clean and well-defined
- Pricing benchmarks against curator economics are compelling (1–2.5% of revenue)
- "10 curators at $20K = $200K ARR" is a credible sustainability target

- **Willingness to pay is asserted, not validated.** Has any curator been asked "would you pay $20K/year for this?" A single confirmed Letter of Interest with pricing discussion is worth more than the entire pricing table.

- **Insurance pricing is mentioned as Phase 2+ but is actually the stronger long-term business.** Curators are cost-sensitive and can churn. Insurers *need* quantitative risk inputs to price policies and will pay actuarial value, not subscription willingness. Consider elevating this.

### Radical challenge — the sustainability model might be upside-down:

Insurance is a larger TAM than curator tooling. The public good (open ratings, dependency graphs) attracts TVL to Morpho, making the ecosystem more insurable, creating the demand for PD estimates. The grant funds the public good; insurance revenue funds the team.

# RFC v0.1 — Revised Structural Critique

## The Central Problem: What Is Morpho Being Asked to Fund?

The RFC currently asks Morpho governance to fund three things bundled as one:

1. **A public transparency layer** (DIG/YTD dependency graphs, open methodology, vault dossiers)
2. **A quantitative scoring system** (Z₁–Z₆, GARCH, HMM, Merton PD — AAA-to-CCC ratings)
3. **The R&D foundation for a private SaaS business** (real-time feeds, curator subscriptions)

These have different governance legitimacy. #1 is a clean public good — no curator will build it alone, it benefits all ecosystem participants, and it's the kind of thing DAOs should fund. #2 is speculative research with weak calibration data and high failure risk. #3 is a for-profit business that happens to need grant capital to bootstrap.

The RFC bundles all three because the team needs all three to work. But governance voters evaluate each differently. A voter who loves #1 may reject the package because #3 makes it look like vendor incubation. A voter who'd fund #2 as pure research may balk at the SaaS tail. **The bundling is the primary political risk, not the wording.**

### The "DAO funds customer acquisition" attack

Anyone on the forum can read the sustainability model and say: "You're asking Morpho to spend $250K building a product whose free tier attracts curators to your paid tier. That's a marketing budget, not a public good." The RFC has no defense against this because the defense requires either:

- **Separating the grant scope from the commercial scope** — fund only the public layer, with the SaaS explicitly out of scope for grant money
- **Or making the commercial model the governance case** — "fund us to build a self-sustaining risk utility; the grant is an investment in ecosystem infrastructure that pays for its own maintenance"

The current draft tries to do both and does neither convincingly.

## Chaos Labs: The Argument That Eats Itself

The Chaos Labs departure validates that risk infrastructure is underserved and underpriced. But it also proves:

- **Risk work is operationally brutal.** Chaos Labs had 40+ people, ran at negative margins at $5M+/year, and still couldn't make the economics work on a $26B protocol. Sigma Labs is proposing to do a subset of this with 2.5 FTE at $250K. Either the scope is genuinely smaller (plausible — transparency layer, not parameter management) or the team is planning to work for free after the grant runs out.
- **Protocol-specific risk infrastructure gets stranded.** Chaos Labs' work was worthless when Aave rewrote its architecture. The RFC claims Morpho's architecture is "stable by design" — but Morpho v2 (intent-based markets, fixed-rate/fixed-term) is explicitly on the roadmap. If v2 changes the vault/curator model, the risk model needs rebuilding. The RFC doesn't address this.
- **Single-client dependency is fatal.** Chaos Labs' revenue depended on one DAO. The sustainability model claims to avoid this via multiple curator subscriptions — but the grant itself is single-client dependency for the critical first 12 months. If the relationship with Morpho governance sours (see: ACI on Aave), the project dies regardless of product quality.

**The honest use of Chaos Labs:** "The largest risk provider in DeFi just failed at $5M/year. We are not proposing to replace them. We are proposing a narrower, cheaper, more defensible contribution — open explainability infrastructure — and we have a plan to sustain it without perpetual DAO funding."

## The Scoring Layer May Be the Wrong First Product

The RFC's highest-risk deliverable is the quantitative scoring system (Z₁–Z₆ → GARCH → HMM → σe → Merton PD → AAA–CCC ratings). Consider what this requires:

- **Calibration data that doesn't exist.** GJR-GARCH(1,1)-t needs enough historical returns data per asset for MLE convergence. Many Morpho collateral assets are < 6 months old. The MAP estimator with category priors is the specified workaround, but *the priors don't exist either* — they're a grant deliverable.
- **Cross-sectional distribution that's thin.** The intrinsic rating (AAA–CCC) requires quantile boundaries from the cross-sectional distribution of σe. With 50+ assets, you get a distribution. With the 30 that have enough data, you get noise. Quarterly recalibration of quantile boundaries on a 30-asset sample is statistically shaky.
- **A backtesting framework validated against three incidents.** Three data points is not validation — it's storytelling. And two of the three (xUSD, Resolv) involve oracle failures, which means Z₅ is doing most of the work. A model that's really just "flag hardcoded oracles and missing timelocks" doesn't need GARCH/HMM/Merton to be useful.
- **The rating language invites attack before evidence exists.** The moment you publish "Asset X: BBB" you become liable for every loss on a BBB-rated asset. Credora already occupies this space with established relationships. Entering the rating game before having calibration evidence is a credibility trap.

### What Morpho may actually need instead:

1. **Explainability.** The wsrUSD yield tree is genuinely novel. No other tool shows a depositor that their "USDC savings" is actually 85.8% BTC exposure across two custodians at 86% LLTV with circular wsrUSD collateral. This is the product that serves user education (what Albist says Morpho wants to fund) *and* curator accountability.

2. **Incident monitoring.** The three case studies show that oracle hardcoding, missing timelocks, and recursive leverage loops are *the* failure modes. You don't need a 6-factor GARCH model to flag these — you need a structured watchdog that alerts when an oracle hasn't updated in 2× its heartbeat, a governance parameter changed without timelock, or leverage loops exceed a threshold.

3. **Curator comparability.** Instead of scoring assets, score *curator decision quality over time.* Which curators had exposure to USD0++ before the depeg? Which ones had xUSD leverage loops? Which ones adjusted LTV after on-chain conditions changed? This is more native to Morpho's curation architecture than asset-level ratings, and it directly serves depositor decision-making.

**None of these require GARCH, HMM, or Merton.** They require DIG/YTD (built) + structured on-chain monitoring (engineering, not research) + a public dashboard (straightforward).

## Budget: The Heroic Labor Problem

The critique isn't that line items are confusing (though they are). It's that the numbers imply one of three things:

1. **The team is planning to work below market rate for 12 months.** $250K / 2.5 FTE / 12 months = $8,333/person/month. For quant researchers from Polytechnique/ENS with DeFi protocol experience, this is 30–40% of market. Sustainable only if team members have other income streams (they do — Ellen Capital, treasury management, CNRS). But then the "2.5 FTE" claim is aspirational, not operational. The real capacity is probably 1–1.5 FTE of focused attention.

2. **The milestone plan assumes heroic execution.** Z₁–Z₃ on testnet in 2 months, full 6-factor + GARCH in 4, σe and ratings in 6, Merton PD + dashboard in 8, audit in 10, mainnet in 12. Each milestone individually is plausible; all twelve in sequence with 1.5 effective FTE is a stretch. One sick month, one calibration dead end, one audit delay, and the timeline collapses.

3. **There's unpaid labor baked in.** The DIG/YTD are already built — that's significant pre-grant investment. The math reference v3.0 is written. The team has already done 3–6 months of work for free. The grant partially compensates past work while funding future work. This is fine, but it should be stated explicitly rather than hidden behind a 12-month project framing.

## Credora: The Political Non-Question

The RFC shouldn't position against Credora *or* alongside Credora. It should **make Credora irrelevant to the ask.** The way to do this:

- "We are not proposing a competing rating system. We are proposing open infrastructure — dependency graphs, yield decomposition, structured monitoring — that any rating system, including Credora's, can consume as input. The question for governance is not 'Credora or Sigma Labs' but 'should this structural data layer exist as a public good.'"

This sidesteps the political question entirely and reframes the governance decision around infrastructure, not competing vendors.

## Revised Recommendations

### 1. Split the RFC into two phases with separate governance votes.

**Phase 1 ($100–120K, 6 months):** Public explainability + monitoring layer.
- DIG/YTD open-sourced immediately (already built)
- Structured incident monitoring (oracle staleness, governance mutations, leverage loop detection)
- Vault dossiers for top 30 assets
- Dashboard v1 showing dependency graphs and monitoring alerts
- Curator trial with at least 2 teams

**Phase 2 ($100–130K, 6 months, separate vote after shadow-mode evidence):** Quantitative scoring layer.
- Z₁–Z₆ factor scoring, calibration, backtesting
- σe, intrinsic rating, PD estimates
- Conditional on: Phase 1 delivered, curator feedback positive, shadow-mode results published

This reduces governance risk (smaller first ask), creates accountability checkpoints, avoids rating-language credibility traps before calibration exists, and gives the community evidence before committing to the research-heavy second half.

### 2. Require demand validation, not just Letters of Interest.

A Letter of Interest is "yes, that sounds nice." What the RFC needs:

- **A co-funding commitment:** "Curator X will contribute $Y toward Phase 1 if the DAO matches." Even $10K from one curator transforms the governance optics from "fund our research" to "match demonstrated ecosystem demand."
- **Or a trial commitment:** "Curator X will deploy DIG/YTD in their risk workflow for 90 days and publish an evaluation." This costs the curator nothing but reputation, and it gives governance voters a named stakeholder with skin in the game.

### 3. Reframe sustainability as "infrastructure that earns its own maintenance."

Don't present the paid tier as the business model. Present it as the maintenance funding mechanism for the public good:

- "The grant builds public infrastructure. The professional tier exists to fund ongoing maintenance, recalibration, and coverage expansion — so the DAO doesn't need to fund perpetual renewals. If the professional tier fails to reach sustainability within 18 months of Phase 1 delivery, the team commits to transitioning maintenance to a community-run model and open-sourcing all operational tooling."

This transforms "DAO funds my SaaS" into "DAO funds infrastructure with a built-in sustainability mechanism and an explicit exit plan."

### 4. Consider the inversion: score curators, not assets.

This is the most radical suggestion but the most Morpho-native. Morpho's architecture says: *curators are the risk layer.* A tool that rates curators by decision quality — exposure concentration, reaction time to on-chain signals, historical incident exposure, LTV aggressiveness relative to collateral volatility — directly serves depositor decision-making and reinforces the protocol's design philosophy. It doesn't compete with Credora (which rates assets), doesn't require GARCH calibration data, and produces immediate legibility for the retail users Morpho says it wants to serve.

### 5. Open-source DIG/YTD now, not at month 12.

The strongest proof-of-work the team has is already built. Releasing it today:
- Demonstrates good faith to governance voters
- Creates community engagement before the vote
- Lets curators trial the tools during the feedback window
- Makes the "public good" framing credible on day one, not a post-delivery promise

The risk of being scooped is low — the tools are useful but not revenue-generating without the operational layer. The reputational upside of open-sourcing pre-vote vastly outweighs the competitive risk.

The fundamental question for the next draft: **Is this an infrastructure grant or a research grant?** If infrastructure, lead with DIG/YTD, monitoring, and explainability — things that work today and serve users now. If research, lead with the model and accept the calibration timeline risk. The current draft tries to be both and satisfies neither constituency fully.
