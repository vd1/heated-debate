# Context: Individual QSL researcher participation in ProQure

## Previous debate conclusions (already established)

- QSL as an institution does not bid into ProQure — accepted
- Kashefi (QSL Director + NQCC Chief Scientist) excluded from ProQure calls — accepted
- QSL's evaluation role: technical evidence provider, never scores/ranks/recommends
- Governance: published methodology, frozen protocols, red-route conflict management, capability escrow, process assurance
- Dedicated evaluation budget needed (£2-3M Phase 1) — QSL cannot evaluate for free
- Separation is between evidence and decisions, not between QSL and vendors

## The discrimination argument

Other UK academics are permitted to act as subcontractors to ProQure vendors:
- Researchers from quantum technology hubs (funded by EPSRC, just like QSL)
- Academics from Oxford, UCL, Imperial, Bristol, etc.
- Industry researchers from companies not bidding as primes

These academics often hold their own EPSRC grants and government funding. QSL's QATCH funding is not structurally different from other quantum hubs' funding. The only difference is that QSL also has the TTE evaluation role.

**The question:** Why should a QSL researcher be excluded from helping a ProQure vendor simply because they are employed by QSL, when an equally-funded researcher at another university doing similar work can participate?

## The tension

If QSL researcher X acts as subcontractor to ProQure vendor A, while QSL also evaluates vendor A:
- Researcher X has insider knowledge of vendor A's system
- QSL's evaluation of vendor A could be influenced by X's knowledge
- But the red-route model already handles this: X is excluded from evaluating vendor A

The question is whether the red-route (person-specific, subsystem-specific conflict management) is sufficient, or whether DSIT will insist on institutional-level exclusion regardless.

## The line-drawing problem

- **Kashefi:** excluded from ProQure — she is QSL Director, NQCC Chief Scientist, and leads the evaluation function (WP 3.1). Her involvement in a vendor bid would be indefensible.
- **WP 3.x leads (Roffe, García-Patrón):** their expertise (error correction, resource estimation) is closest to the evaluation function. Participation is possible but requires strong governance.
- **WP 1.x and 2.x leads (Wallden, Doosti, Thomson, Brown, Arapinis, Heunen):** their expertise (algorithms, ML, simulation, HPC, security, programming languages) is further from the evaluation function. Exclusion seems disproportionate.
- **Sector leads (Michel, Khamseh, Szpruch, Dent, Belle, Danos):** their expertise (chemistry, genomics, finance, energy, AI) is domain-specific and essentially orthogonal to hardware evaluation. Exclusion makes no sense.

## What DSIT needs to hear

- Blanket exclusion of QSL researchers is discriminatory and counterproductive
- The UK's best quantum expertise should not be locked out of its largest quantum programme
- Person-specific conflict management (red-route) is more proportionate than institutional exclusion
- The line should be drawn by function (evaluation vs. non-evaluation), not by employer
