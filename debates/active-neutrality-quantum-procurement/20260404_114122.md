### Premise attack: active neutrality may be self-undermining

The paragraph assumes deep technical engagement and evaluative neutrality are compatible. Three reasons they may not be:

**Epistemic capture.** Co-developing verification techniques with vendors means co-constructing the evaluation criteria. The institution doesn't just assess — it shapes the framework through its own technical choices, which inevitably reflect bets about which approaches are promising. This is not evaluation from the outside; it's evaluation entangled with the thing being evaluated.

**Relational asymmetry.** Even-handedness requires equal engagement across all vendors. But vendors differ in openness, maturity, and willingness to collaborate. The institution will engage more deeply with more open or more mature vendors, creating structural bias that favors incumbents or those who game the co-development relationship. "Active neutrality across all substrates" is an ideal that breaks on contact with unequal access.

**Scientific process is not a neutral arena.** "Participating in the open scientific process" sounds impartial, but quantum computing research groups are often funded by the very vendors being evaluated. The institution's engagement with the scientific community would be engagement with a field already shaped by vendor money and vendor priorities.

### Weakest claims — cut or rewrite

1. **"Structurally blind" / "not knowing much."** This is a straw man. Passive neutrality in procurement law is procedural fairness — arm's length, documented, auditable. It exists for legal reasons: it creates a defensible record and limits surface area for challenge. Dismissing it as ignorance misrepresents the actual alternative.

2. **"No amount of paperwork review can adjudicate."** False binary. The alternative to active engagement is not "paperwork." It could be independent academic panels, competitive benchmarking protocols, structured vendor demonstrations, or staged pilots. The paragraph collapses a spectrum of options into two poles.

3. **"Authority and discernment."** Vague. Authority in procurement comes from legal mandate, procedural integrity, and defensible methodology — not from institutional charisma or technical fluency. This claim needs grounding.

### Strongest claims — keep and sharpen

1. **The passive/active distinction itself.** Genuinely useful. The insight that different modes of neutrality produce different institutional capabilities is sound and worth developing.

2. **The pre-consolidation argument.** Correct that quantum hardware procurement faces a challenge most frontier procurements don't: the science is unsettled. But the paragraph draws the wrong inference (see below).

3. **Procedural neutrality alone may not produce good technical outcomes.** Plausible. Worth arguing from evidence rather than assertion.

### Missing arguments — needed for credibility

**1. Governance architecture.** The paragraph proposes an institutional model without any governance mechanism. What makes active neutrality survivable?
   - Funding independence: who pays, and how is the funder prevented from influencing outcomes?
   - Recusal rules: when engagement with a vendor crosses from evaluation into partnership, who calls it?
   - Expert rotation and term limits to prevent relational entrenchment
   - Disclosure requirements for all vendor interactions
   - Benchmark IP ownership: who controls co-developed verification techniques?
   - Appeals process for vendors who contest the evaluation
   - Audit trail compatible with procurement law

Without these, "active neutrality" is a slogan, not an institutional design.

**2. Precedent or analogy.** Has something like active neutrality been tried? CERN procurement involves deep technical engagement with suppliers across competing approaches. ESA contractor selection, DARPA evaluation panels, and NIST post-quantum cryptography standardization all represent variations on the spectrum between passive and active evaluation. The paragraph needs at least one concrete comparison to show this model is feasible — or to learn from where it failed.

**3. The alternative architecture question.** The paragraph treats the choice as binary: passive silo vs. active institution. But the pre-consolidation argument could equally support:
   - **Modular procurement / staged pilots** — buy small-scale from multiple vendors, evaluate in-house, scale winners
   - **Portfolio approach** — deliberately procure across substrates, treating it as a diversified bet rather than a winner-take-all decision
   - **Delayed standardization** — invest in substrate-agnostic software/middleware, defer hardware lock-in until the science settles
   - **Independent red-teaming** — commission evaluations from rotating academic groups with conflict disclosure, without institutional co-development

If the science is genuinely unsettled, perhaps the right response is not to build a better adjudicator but to avoid premature adjudication entirely.

**4. Legal defensibility and procurement timelines.** Active engagement with vendors creates legal surface for challenges. Any losing vendor can argue the institution's co-development with a competitor biased the evaluation. The paragraph ignores that procurement operates under legal frameworks (EU directive 2014/24/EU, US FAR) designed around procedural fairness. Active neutrality needs to explain how it survives legal scrutiny, not just scientific scrutiny.

**5. State capacity.** Who staffs this institution? Quantum hardware expertise is scarce and overwhelmingly employed by the vendors being evaluated. The paragraph assumes the institution can attract enough independent talent to maintain "high-coverage engagement across all vendors and substrates." This is a workforce problem that needs addressing, not assuming away.

### Counterfactual test

If active neutrality is rejected, the strongest alternative is probably **staged modular procurement with independent evaluation**:
- Define functional requirements, not substrate requirements
- Issue small-scale contracts to multiple vendors across substrates
- Evaluate delivered systems against standardized benchmarks run by a rotating independent panel
- Scale up based on demonstrated performance, not predicted capability
- Build replaceability into contracts so early picks can be reversed

This avoids capture, is legally defensible, respects the unsettled science, and doesn't require an institution with superhuman even-handedness.

### Recommended rewrite priorities

1. **Drop the straw man.** Rewrite the passive-neutrality characterization as a serious model with real advantages (legal defensibility, auditability) and specific limitations — not as an absurdity.
2. **Add governance.** Even two sentences on safeguards (rotation, disclosure, recusal, funding independence) would transform the proposal from a slogan to a sketch of institutional design.
3. **Engage the alternative architectures.** Acknowledge that staged pilots or portfolio procurement are real competitors to the single-institution model, and explain why active neutrality is preferable to those approaches.
4. **Ground the pre-consolidation argument.** Instead of "no amount of paperwork can adjudicate," specify what an evaluation body would actually need to assess (e.g., qubit coherence times under operational conditions, error-correction overhead, fabrication yield) and why those assessments require engagement rather than documentation review.
5. **Add a precedent.** One concrete example of a comparable institution that succeeded or failed at active neutrality under analogous conditions.
6. **Address the self-undermining risk directly.** Acknowledge that deep engagement creates capture risk and explain the specific mechanism by which the institution prevents it. Don't pretend even-handedness is self-enforcing.
